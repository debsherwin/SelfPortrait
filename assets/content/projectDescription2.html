Problem

Ceres Imaging collects aerial imagery of agricultural land to provide bird’s-eye views of crop health and irrigation problems. To-date, these analytic assessments were delivered to the farm manager in a static image format—a format that could only be ‘read’ by a trained human who could ‘eyeball’ problem patterns or trends.

Goals

  • Use a vector-based map to unlock the quantified data in the aerial imagery
  • Enable an untrained user to identify salient patterns and trends
  • Visualize the impact of actions taken in-field
  • Aggregate insights across fields

Process

This paradigm shift in Ceres’s customer-facing deliverable was developed in several phases over multiple growing seasons. In all phases, my role was to align our dataset with the real-world management workflows of our farm managers.

In the first phase, I conducted discovery sessions to understand the key points of their workflow. Identifying high variability within a field, was a priority. So when we introduced vector-based map layers with tree- and vine-level data, these layers came packaged with a dynamic ‘histo-legend’ that a) described the metric b) displayed the distribution and c) acted as a clickable filter to find and count the most-stressed plants.

We contained the “histo-legend” in a flexible panel that could be progressively revealed, or made compact, depending on screen size or the scope of data in the view.

User feedback was overwhelmingly positive: the bar chart view was easy to understand at a glance and could be communicated clearly to upper management. But it was still difficult to compare one distribution to another, to understand if field actions effectively improved crop health.

So in the next phase—in addition to supporting a wider variety of crops with a variety of new data granularities—we curated charts to highlight important comparisons. Water Stress is expected to change week-to-week, so a ‘Tornado chart” illustrates short-term changes. Vegetative metrics follow a slow curve, so they have a time series of the field average that can be compared year-over-year.

This feature set was also well-received by farm managers, but the value was bottle-necked by the fact that the user had to navigate through individual farms and fields to find the direction of each trend. Our response was to aggregate and rank field- and zone-level in the map view and on a dedicated summary page.

Outcome

Farm managers across four continents are able to analyze millions of acres, enabling them to precisely target water, fertilizer, and labor.
